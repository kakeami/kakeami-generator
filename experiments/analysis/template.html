1. Introduction

Kakeami (カケアミ) is a shading technique in Japanese manga. Small blocks — approximately 7–8 mm squares — are filled with parallel hatching lines, and adjacent blocks use different angles to create tonal gradation (Pixiv Encyclopedia; Toudo, 2003). The technique has a hierarchy of density: 1-kake (single direction), 2-kake (perpendicular overlay at 90°), 3-kake (adding a 45° diagonal), and 4-kake (four directions at 45° intervals).

Unlike Western cross-hatching, where long strokes traverse entire regions along contour or principal curvature directions, kakeami uses a fragmented patch structure: each tile is a small, self-contained unit whose orientation is chosen to contrast with its neighbours (Japanese with Anime).

Despite its importance in manga production, no academic paper has formally defined kakeami or evaluated automatic generation algorithms quantitatively. This report presents the first mathematical formulation of kakeami quality and a controlled 2×2 factorial experiment evaluating two independent components of a proposed algorithm: tile placement (Poisson-disk vs. uniform random) and angle assignment (BFS greedy vs. random).

The remainder of the report is organised as follows. Section 2 reviews related work in NPR hatching and manga generation to identify the gap this work addresses. Section 3 introduces a novel mathematical formulation for kakeami quality, drawing on concepts from nematic liquid crystal physics and direction field theory. Section 4 describes the proposed algorithm and the baseline conditions. Section 5 details the experimental setup, Section 6 presents the quantitative results including two-way ANOVA and effect size analysis, Section 7 discusses their implications, and Section 8 outlines directions for future work.

2. Related Work

To situate our contribution, we survey three lines of prior work: classical NPR hatching, ML-based manga generation, and aperiodic tiling theory. In each case, we highlight what existing approaches optimise and where kakeami-specific generation remains unaddressed.

2.1 NPR Hatching

Praun et al. (2001) introduced Tonal Art Maps for real-time hatching in non-photorealistic rendering (NPR). Their approach aligns strokes along principal curvature directions, prioritising smoothness. Hertzmann & Zorin (2000) similarly optimise direction fields for illustrative hatching on 3D surfaces. Our approach inverts this paradigm: kakeami requires contrast maximisation between adjacent tiles, making neighbouring angles as different as possible.

2.2 ML-Based Manga Tone Generation

Recent work uses machine learning for manga screentone generation: ScreenVAE (Xie et al., SIGGRAPH Asia 2020), Zhang et al. (CVPR 2021), and Sketch2Manga (2024). These are data-driven and provide limited user control over geometric parameters. A purely geometric, controllable approach to kakeami-specific hatching remains unexplored.

2.3 Aperiodic Tilings in NPR

The Pinwheel tiling (Conway & Radin, 1998) provides aperiodic tessellations with infinite rotational diversity. While studied in mathematical tiling theory, no prior application to NPR hatching has been reported.

2.4 Direction Field Design

In geometry processing, direction fields on meshes are optimised using Ginzburg–Landau energy functionals (Beaufort et al., 2017; Viertel & Osting, 2019), primarily for quad meshing rather than artistic hatching. The connection between direction fields and nematic liquid crystals is well known in physics (both deal with headless directors on manifolds), but no prior work applies this framework to evaluate or generate kakeami patterns.

2.5 Our Contribution

The combination of Poisson-disk sampling for tile placement, Voronoi adjacency graph construction, and BFS greedy angle optimisation has no precedent in the literature. This report provides the first quantitative ablation study of these components using quality metrics newly proposed for kakeami evaluation.

3. Formulation

The related work reveals a clear gap: no formal framework exists for defining what makes a kakeami pattern “good.” In this section, we propose a novel mathematical formulation that addresses this gap. The data structures (Section 3.2) mirror the manual drawing process, while the quality metrics (Section 3.3) are original contributions of this work, drawing inspiration from nematic liquid crystal physics and direction field theory to capture the key perceptual properties of kakeami — local angular contrast, anti-ferromagnetic ordering, and global isotropy.

3.1 Angle Space

A fundamental observation is that hatching lines at 0° and 180° are visually identical. This motivates modelling orientations on the real projective line: $\Omega_\theta = \mathbb{RP}^1 \cong [0, \pi)$, with the Fubini–Study distance $d_{\mathbb{RP}^1}(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \min(|\theta_1 - \theta_2| \bmod \pi,\; \pi - |\theta_1 - \theta_2| \bmod \pi)$. This identification, standard in the $N$-RoSy direction field literature (Vaxman et al., 2016), is what enables us to define meaningful distance-based quality metrics below.

3.2 Data Structure Hierarchy

We define a hierarchy that mirrors the manual kakeami drawing process:

  • A Stroke $\sigma = (\theta, \delta)$ specifies an orientation $\theta \in \mathbb{RP}^1$ and a line pitch $\delta > 0$.
  • A Block $\beta$ groups a primary stroke with optional secondary strokes (for $k$-kake layering).
  • A Tile $\tau = (c, \beta)$ places a block at centre $c \in \mathbb{R}^2$.
  • A KakeamiConfig $K = \{\tau_1, \ldots, \tau_n\}$ is a finite set of tiles covering a rectangular region.

3.3 Quality Metrics

Given a configuration $K$ and its adjacency edge set $E$ (Voronoi adjacency edges derived from Delaunay triangulation of tile centres), we propose six complementary metrics split across two axes: four angular metrics evaluating orientation quality and two spatial metrics evaluating placement quality. Together they provide a comprehensive quantitative evaluation framework. To the best of our knowledge, these metrics have not been previously defined for hatching evaluation.

Angular Metrics

Angle Contrast $E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$

The most direct measure of kakeami quality: the normalised mean RP¹ distance over all adjacency edges. $$E_{\mathrm{contrast}} = \frac{2}{\pi |E|} \sum_{(i,j) \in E} d_{\mathbb{RP}^1}(\phi_i, \phi_j) \;\in [0, 1]$$ where $\phi_i$ is the primary angle of tile $i$. Higher values indicate better angle separation between neighbours, which is the defining visual characteristic of kakeami.

Landau–de Gennes Energy $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$

Inspired by the nematic liquid crystal order parameter and the Ginzburg–Landau energy functional used in cross field design (Beaufort et al., 2017), we define a discrete analogue: $$E_{\mathrm{LdG}} = \frac{1}{|E|} \sum_{(i,j) \in E} \sin^2(\phi_i - \phi_j) \;\in [0, 1]$$ This measures the tendency towards anti-ferromagnetic ordering on the adjacency graph. $E_{\mathrm{LdG}} = 0$ indicates uniform alignment (all tiles parallel); $E_{\mathrm{LdG}} = 0.5$ corresponds to random orientations; $E_{\mathrm{LdG}} > 0.5$ indicates active angle repulsion beyond random — desirable for kakeami.

Scalar Order Parameter $S$

Borrowed from nematic liquid crystal physics, $S$ measures global orientational order: $$S = \left| \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} e^{2i\phi_i} \right| \;\in [0, 1]$$ $S = 0$ indicates perfect isotropy (all angles equally represented); $S = 1$ indicates all tiles share the same angle. For kakeami, low $S$ is desirable — we want diverse, well-distributed angles rather than a dominant orientation.

Angle Entropy $H_{\mathrm{angle}}$

The normalised Shannon entropy of the primary-angle distribution measures how diverse the angle usage is across all tiles: $$H_{\mathrm{angle}} = -\frac{1}{\ln N_{\mathrm{bins}}} \sum_{b=1}^{N_{\mathrm{bins}}} p_b \ln p_b \;\in [0, 1]$$ where the angle range $[0, \pi)$ is divided into $N_{\mathrm{bins}} = 12$ equal bins (15° each) and $p_b$ is the fraction of tiles whose primary angle falls in bin $b$. $H_{\mathrm{angle}} = 1$ indicates a perfectly uniform distribution of angles across all bins; $H_{\mathrm{angle}} = 0$ means all tiles share the same angle. A checkerboard pattern using only 0° and 90° achieves $H_{\mathrm{angle}} = \ln 2 / \ln 12 \approx 0.28$, revealing its limited angle diversity despite achieving high scores on all other angular metrics.

Spatial Metrics

Coverage Ratio $C_{\mathrm{cov}}$

The fraction of the region covered by at least one tile: $$C_{\mathrm{cov}} = \frac{|\{p \in R : p \in \bigcup_i \tau_i\}|}{|R|} \;\in [0, 1]$$ Estimated by sampling the region on a regular grid and testing point-in-rotated-rectangle membership. $C_{\mathrm{cov}} = 1$ means no background is visible; lower values indicate gaps due to tile clustering. This metric is standard in computational geometry as packing density and in NPR stippling as coverage ratio (Secord, 2002).

Voronoi Cell Area CV $U_{\mathrm{vor}}$

The coefficient of variation (CV) of Voronoi cell areas measures spatial uniformity of tile placement: $$U_{\mathrm{vor}} = \frac{\sigma_A}{\bar{A}}$$ where $A_i$ is the area of the Voronoi cell for tile $i$, clipped to the region boundary. $U_{\mathrm{vor}} = 0$ for a perfect lattice (all cells identical); $U_{\mathrm{vor}} \approx 0.53$ for a Poisson process (completely random); Poisson-disk sampling yields intermediate values. This is a standard measure in spatial statistics for point pattern analysis (Okabe et al., Spatial Tessellations, 2000). Lower values indicate more uniform tile distribution.

4. Method

With the quality metrics defined above, we now describe the proposed algorithm and the baseline conditions designed to isolate the contribution of each component. The algorithm has two stages — tile placement and angle assignment — and we compare two strategies for each, yielding a 2×2 factorial design.

4.1 Algorithm Pipeline Overview

Figure 1 illustrates the three stages of the proposed method: (1) Poisson-disk sampling generates well-spaced tile centres, (2) Delaunay triangulation constructs the Voronoi adjacency graph, and (3) BFS greedy traversal assigns angles that maximise local contrast.

4.2 Tile Placement Strategies

Poisson-disk (Bridson's algorithm): generates well-spaced points with a minimum distance guarantee ($r = \texttt{tileSize}$), producing blue noise spatial distribution. The resulting points serve as tile centres, and a Voronoi adjacency graph is constructed via Delaunay triangulation.

Uniform random: $n$ points drawn from $\mathrm{Uniform}([x_{\min}, x_{\max}] \times [y_{\min}, y_{\max}])$. The count $n$ is matched to the Poisson-disk output for the same seed, ensuring a fair comparison.

4.3 Angle Assignment Strategies

BFS greedy: Starting from the highest-degree node in the adjacency graph, a breadth-first search visits all nodes. For each visited node, 360 candidate angles $\{0, \pi/360, 2\pi/360, \ldots\}$ are evaluated, and the angle maximising $\min_{j \in N_{\mathrm{placed}}} d_{\mathbb{RP}^1}(\theta, \phi_j)$ is selected.

Random: each tile independently receives $\theta \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0, \pi)$.

In addition to the 2×2 factorial conditions, we include a fifth condition: Grid + Checkerboard (gridCheckerboard). Tiles are placed on a regular square grid with spacing equal to the tile size (0.6), yielding $10 \times 10 = 100$ tiles in the $[0, 6]^2$ region. Angles alternate between 0° and 90° in a checkerboard pattern: tiles at even $(r + c)$ positions receive 0°, odd positions receive 90°. This condition is entirely deterministic (identical across all seeds) and serves to demonstrate the checkerboard hack: a simple regular pattern that achieves near-optimal scores on $E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$, $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$, $S$, $C_{\mathrm{cov}}$, and $U_{\mathrm{vor}}$ while producing a visually mechanical, non-natural result. The angle entropy $H_{\mathrm{angle}}$ is specifically designed to penalise this configuration.

Note that gridCheckerboard uses 100 tiles (vs. ~69 for Poisson-disk conditions) because it uses the natural grid spacing rather than Poisson-disk sampling. This tile count difference is expected and intentional: the condition exists to demonstrate a metric vulnerability, not for fair competition.

7. Discussion

The results in Section 6 reveal clear performance differences among the four conditions. We now analyse the individual and combined effects of the two algorithmic components using the ANOVA results, interpret the metrics in the context of the nematic analogy introduced in Section 3, and identify limitations.

7.1 Effect of Angle Assignment

The two-way ANOVA confirms that the angle assignment strategy is the dominant factor. For $E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$, the angle factor shows a large effect size ($\eta^2$), indicating that BFS greedy assignment accounts for the majority of variance in angle contrast. The BFS greedy strategy consistently outperforms random angle assignment on both $E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$ and $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$, confirming that local contrast maximisation via the adjacency graph is the primary driver of visual quality.

7.2 Effect of Tile Placement

While the angular metrics ($E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$, $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$, $S$) show little sensitivity to placement strategy, the spatial metrics reveal a stark difference. Poisson-disk placement achieves near-perfect coverage ($C_{\mathrm{cov}} \approx 1$) thanks to the 1.4× tile expansion and minimum-distance guarantee, whereas uniform random placement leaves visible gaps ($C_{\mathrm{cov}} < 1$) due to tile clustering. The Voronoi cell area CV ($U_{\mathrm{vor}}$) confirms this: Poisson-disk conditions show significantly lower CV (more uniform spacing) than random conditions, which approach the theoretical Poisson process value of $\approx 0.53$. These spatial metrics capture a critical quality dimension — visual completeness — that the angular metrics alone cannot detect.

7.3 k-Invariance of Quality Metrics

An important property of all six quality metrics is their invariance to the kake count $k$. All six metrics — $E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$, $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$, $S$, $H_{\mathrm{angle}}$, $C_{\mathrm{cov}}$, and $U_{\mathrm{vor}}$ — depend only on the primary angle $\phi_i$ of each tile, which is determined entirely by the tile placement and angle assignment strategies. The secondary strokes added for $k \geq 2$ (at fixed angular offsets of 45°, 90°, etc.) do not influence any of the metrics.

This $k$-invariance is a limitation of the current metric framework: the metrics cannot distinguish between a $k = 1$ and $k = 4$ configuration that share the same primary angles, even though they look very different visually. We acknowledge this as a gap and propose $k$-aware metrics as future work (Section 8.4).

7.4 Interpretation of $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$

For BFS conditions, $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$ consistently exceeds 0.5, indicating anti-ferromagnetic ordering — adjacent tiles actively repel each other's angles beyond what random chance would produce. Recalling the nematic analogy from Section 3, this means the BFS algorithm creates configurations where neighbouring “directors” tend towards perpendicular alignment, the hallmark of effective kakeami generation.

7.5 Scalar Order Parameter

Low $S$ values for BFS conditions indicate good global isotropy: no single angle dominates. Random angle assignment produces slightly higher $S$ variance, as uniform sampling can accidentally produce correlated angles in small samples.

7.6 Limitations

  • All evaluation is metric-based. A human perceptual study would strengthen the conclusions.
  • The experiment uses a single region size and tile size. Scalability to larger regions has not been tested.
  • The 360-candidate grid for BFS is a discretisation; finer grids or continuous optimisation may improve results marginally.
  • The quality metrics are $k$-invariant: they capture only primary-angle structure and cannot evaluate the visual contribution of secondary hatch layers (see Section 7.3).
  • All conditions use Voronoi adjacency derived from Delaunay triangulation of tile centres. This ensures consistency between the BFS optimisation graph and metric evaluation, but other adjacency definitions (e.g. $k$-nearest-neighbours or radius-based) remain unexplored.
  • The Grid + Checkerboard condition uses a different tile count (100 vs. ~69) than the Poisson-disk conditions due to its regular grid spacing. This prevents direct quantitative comparison of spatial metrics but does not affect its role as a metric-vulnerability demonstration (see Section 7.7).

7.7 The Checkerboard Problem and Angle Entropy

The Grid + Checkerboard condition demonstrates a fundamental vulnerability in the original five-metric framework ($E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$, $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$, $S$, $C_{\mathrm{cov}}$, $U_{\mathrm{vor}}$). By placing tiles on a regular grid and alternating between just two angles (0° and 90°), the checkerboard achieves near-optimal scores on all five metrics simultaneously: perfect edge contrast (every neighbour differs by 90°), maximum $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$ (all $\sin^2$ terms equal 1), low $S$ (the two symmetric angles cancel in the order parameter), full coverage ($C_{\mathrm{cov}} \approx 1$), and zero spatial variation ($U_{\mathrm{vor}} = 0$).

Yet the resulting pattern is visually mechanical and repetitive — clearly inferior to the organic, varied appearance produced by the Poisson + BFS algorithm. The angle entropy $H_{\mathrm{angle}}$ resolves this discrepancy: the checkerboard uses only 2 of 12 angle bins, yielding $H_{\mathrm{angle}} \approx \ln 2 / \ln 12 \approx 0.28$, far below the BFS conditions ($H_{\mathrm{angle}} \approx 0.95$+). This confirms that angle diversity — using a rich variety of orientations, not just two — is essential for natural-looking kakeami, and that $H_{\mathrm{angle}}$ captures a quality dimension the other metrics miss.

Note that the gridCheckerboard condition uses 100 tiles (10×10 grid) compared to ~69 tiles for Poisson-disk conditions. This tile count difference does not affect the validity of the metric comparison: the purpose of this condition is to expose the metric vulnerability, not to compete on equal footing with stochastic conditions. The zero-variance box plots for gridCheckerboard (identical results across all seeds) further emphasise its deterministic, non-natural character.

8. Future Work

The experiment framework and quality metrics established in this report open several directions for future research.

8.1 Tonal Gradation via Spatially Varying $k$

In traditional manga practice, artists create smooth tonal gradients by varying the kake count $k$ across a region — transitioning, for example, from 1-kake (lightest) to 4-kake (darkest) to depict the curvature of a face or the fall of shadow. Our current implementation uses a uniform $k$ across all tiles. A natural extension is to accept a scalar field $k(x, y)$ that maps spatial position to kake count, enabling automatic kakeami gradation. The quality metrics defined in Section 3 would need to be extended to handle heterogeneous $k$ values, e.g. by weighting edge contrast contributions by the local $k$.

8.2 Generalisation to Arbitrary Pattern Tiles

A key insight of this work is that the proposed algorithm is pattern-agnostic along two independent axes: the fill content drawn inside each tile, and the tile shape itself. The Poisson-disk placement, Voronoi adjacency construction, and BFS greedy angle assignment operate solely on tile centres and primary angles — neither the rendering primitive nor the tile boundary geometry participates in the optimisation. This opens two complementary directions for generalisation.

8.2.1 Non-Line Fill Patterns in Rectangular Tiles

The rectangular tile shape can be preserved while replacing straight-line hatching with a fundamentally different fill primitive. For example, placing dots along the hatch-line positions at regular intervals produces a stipple pattern whose directional alignment reveals the tile’s primary angle without drawing any continuous line. Other candidates include small crosses, short dashes, concentric arcs, or any oriented motif. Because the fill is independent of the placement framework, the same quality metrics ($E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$, $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$, $S$) remain valid: they depend only on the primary angle $\phi_i$ of each tile, not on how that angle is visually manifested inside the tile.

8.2.2 Non-Rectangular Tile Shapes

Independently of the fill content, the tile shape itself can be generalised. Our algorithm already computes a Voronoi partition internally to determine tile adjacency; the Voronoi cells themselves are natural, non-rectangular tile candidates. Each Voronoi cell is an individual kakeami unit: the BFS greedy algorithm assigns an angle to each cell, and the cell polygon is hatched directly — without grouping multiple non-rectangular shapes into a rectangular container. Other tile geometries (regular hexagons, triangles, Penrose rhombi, etc.) are equally viable, as long as an adjacency graph can be constructed.

Together, these two axes of generalisation suggest that the quality metrics and optimisation framework introduced in this work are applicable to a broad class of oriented tile-based texture synthesis problems — any setting where a surface must be covered by directed pattern units with maximal local angular contrast.

8.3 Nawaami (Curved-Line Hatching)

Nawaami (ナワアミ, also called kakenawa カケナワ, literally “rope net”) is a related manga technique that replaces straight hatching lines with curved, sinuous strokes bundled within trapezoidal guide shapes. These curved bundles chain together to form flowing, rope-like textures that convey emotional qualities distinct from standard kakeami — particularly anxiety, unease, and foreboding (Emiya Manga Class). Extending our generation algorithm to nawaami would require replacing the straight-line hatch primitives with parametric curves (e.g. sine-modulated paths or splines) while preserving the tile-based placement and adjacency-optimised angle assignment. The quality metrics could be adapted by defining an analogous RP¹ distance on the tangent direction of curved strokes at tile boundaries.

8.4 Connection to Mathematical Tiling Problems

The tile placement problem studied in this report has deep connections to classical tiling and tessellation theory. The Voronoi-based partition generated by Poisson-disk sampling creates an aperiodic space-filling coverage of the plane analogous to well-known aperiodic tilings such as Penrose tilings and Wang tiles (Grünbaum & Shephard, 1987). Unlike periodic lattice arrangements, the blue-noise property of Poisson-disk sampling ensures that no translational symmetry exists, while the minimum distance guarantee provides a regularity constraint comparable to the matching rules of aperiodic prototile sets.

The angle assignment step can be viewed through the lens of graph colouring: given the Voronoi adjacency graph, the BFS greedy algorithm assigns “colours” (continuous angles in $\mathbb{RP}^1$) to vertices such that adjacent colours are maximally separated. This is analogous to edge-matching constraints in Wang tiles, where adjacent tiles must agree on their shared edge colour, except that our constraint is repulsive rather than attractive — we maximise rather than minimise the distance between neighbouring labels.

The quality metrics defined in Section 3 formalise these “tiling rules” in a continuous angle space. $E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$ measures the mean edge mismatch (analogous to the fraction of satisfied constraints in a constraint satisfaction problem), while $E_{\mathrm{LdG}}$ connects to the Ginzburg–Landau energy functional used in nematic liquid crystal theory and cross-field design for quad meshing (Beaufort et al., 2017; Viertel & Osting, 2019). Conway & Radin (1998) showed that quaquaversal tilings can achieve infinite rotational diversity in 3D tessellations; our BFS greedy algorithm achieves an analogous property in 2D by distributing angles as uniformly as possible (low $S$).

Exploring these connections more rigorously — for instance, proving bounds on $E_{\mathrm{contrast}}$ for Poisson-Voronoi graphs, or relating the angle assignment problem to known results in circular chromatic number theory — is a promising direction for future theoretical work.

8.5 $k$-Aware Quality Metrics

As discussed in Section 7.3, the current metrics are $k$-invariant — they depend only on the primary angle and cannot evaluate the visual contribution of secondary hatch layers. Future work should develop metrics that capture $k$-dependent properties, such as the visual density contrast between adjacent tiles at different $k$ values, the uniformity of angular coverage within each tile, or perceptual texture similarity measures (e.g. SSIM-based comparisons between generated and reference kakeami patterns at each $k$ level).
